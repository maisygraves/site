# Definitions

A *positive* classification is a *default* classification. A *false positive* prediction is the model’s prediction that a borrower will default when, in reality, they don’t. A *true negative* is the inverse - a no default prediction matches reality and a borrower stays current with payments. There are also true positives and false negatives.

*Protected characteristics* from the [ECOA and FHA](https://www.occ.gov/topics/consumers-and-communities/consumer-protection/fair-lending/index-fair-lending.html) include race, color, religion, national origin, sex, familial status, age, handicap, and receipt of income from a public assistance program. In this analysis, a *characteristic* is a classification within a *subgroup*. For instance, 'male' is a characteristic of the 'gender' subgroup.

Bias in the [post Dodd-Frank](https://www.civicresearchinstitute.com/online/PDF/Fair%20Lending%20Laws%20and%20UDAAP%20TFI%202802.pdf) world could be several things. *Disparate impact* is when a bank applies a facially neutral policy/practice to all credit applicants but that policy/practice disproportionately excludes/burdens those with protected characteristics. *Disparate treatment* is when a lender treats a credit applicant differently based on one of the protected characteristics. Proof of motivated prejudice or discrimination are not required for regulatory enforcement, only that it occurred.  

[According to the Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/pubs/bulletin/1996/796lead.pdf), *default* apples to any of the following four situations:

1. forced foreclosure on a mortgage to gain title to the property and secure the loan.
2. lender obtaining the title from the borrower in lieu of foreclosure.
3. the borrower sells the home and lender receives less than full payment on the mortgage obligation.
4. lender and borrower renegotiate the terms of the loan and some or all of the delinquent principal and interest payments are forgiven. 

*Note:* This audit happened right before the Trump Administration [terminated disparate impact liability](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/fair-lending/) in 2025.

# Analysis
McNemar’s test

null hypothesis: there is no difference between true predictions and false predictions. False predictions are unrelated to algorithm logic 

alternative hypothesis: there is a significant difference between true predictions and false predictions. The algorithm’s logic is involved in false predictions.

## Known Limitations in Historical Lending Data

The data is limited to applicants who were approved for a loan by the lending firm. There are two potential exclusions that have an unknown affect on the generalizability of the data to the population:
* application for a loan requires the borrowers’ awareness of and demand for the product (subject to marketing practices and macroeconomic factors).
* actual outcomes necessarily exclude applicants who were denied funds.

The ripple effects of historical discrimination have confounding impacts on economic and social data and is [well documented](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00146-023-01676-3). These impacts are inextricable from data about individual borrowers. Understanding primary default drivers requires continued research and understanding.

# Conclusion

False predictions are likely a result of limited data about default cases. In general, borrowers who default are the minority. This leads to a predictable imbalance in positive and negative cases.

In cases of uncertainty, the algorithm is more likely to give a false positive than a false negative prediction. This tendency might be the reason why we rejected the null hypothesis for so many characteristics. It is also the reason why the model accuracy is so high.

## Recommendations

Mortgage lenders, policy makers, and academics have been researching the drivers of default for decades, and algorithmic fairness will continue to evolve given the complexity and range of the issues. 

The model’s results should inform lending decisions but not lead them. Continued investment in understanding both the drivers of default and industry methods for fair algorithmic decisions. 

An example of investment in understanding the drivers of default would be increasing risk tolerance for borrowers that might default. This would decrease the accuracy of the model and sacrifice profit, but it would also expand the data pool about potential defaulters that are normally excluded from funds.
