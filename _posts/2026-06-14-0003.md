# Definitions

A *positive* classification is a *default* classification. A *false positive* prediction is when the model says that a borrower will default on their loan when they actually don't. 

A *true negative* is the inverse - a *no default* prediction matches reality and a borrower stays current with their payments. There are also true positives and false negatives.

*Protected characteristics* from the [ECOA and FHA](https://www.occ.gov/topics/consumers-and-communities/consumer-protection/fair-lending/index-fair-lending.html) include race, color, religion, national origin, sex, familial status, age, handicap, and receipt of income from a public assistance program. In this analysis, a *characteristic* is a classification within a *subgroup*. For instance, 'male' is a characteristic of the 'gender' subgroup.

*Bias* in the [post Dodd-Frank](https://www.civicresearchinstitute.com/online/PDF/Fair%20Lending%20Laws%20and%20UDAAP%20TFI%202802.pdf) world refers to several disparate outcomes. *Disparate impact* is when a bank applies a facially neutral policy/practice to all credit applicants but that policy/practice disproportionately excludes/burdens those with protected characteristics. *Disparate treatment* is when a lender treats a credit applicant differently based on one of the protected characteristics. Proof of motivated prejudice or discrimination are **not** required for regulatory enforcement, only that it occurred.  

[According to the Federal Reserve](https://www.federalreserve.gov/pubs/bulletin/1996/796lead.pdf), *default* applies to any of the following four situations:

1. the lender forces a foreclosure on a mortgage to gain the title to the property and secure the loan.
2. the lender obtains the title from the borrower in lieu of foreclosure.
3. the borrower sells the home and the lender receives less than full payment on the mortgage obligation.
4. the lender and borrower renegotiate the terms of the loan and some/all of the delinquent principal and interest payments are forgiven. 

*Note:* This audit happened right before the Trump Administration [terminated disparate impact liability](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/fair-lending/) in 2025.

# Analysis
McNemar’s test is a hypothesis test:

* null hypothesis: there is no difference between true predictions and false predictions. False predictions are unrelated to algorithm logic 

* alternative hypothesis: there is a significant difference between true predictions and false predictions. The algorithm’s logic is involved in false predictions.

*Essentially we're answering this question - when the model is wrong, is it the model's thought process or something else?*

### Known Limitations of Historical Lending Data

The data is limited to applicants who were approved for a loan by the lending firm. There are two potential exclusions that have an unknown affect on the generalizability of the data to the population:

* Application for a loan requires the borrowers’ awareness of and demand for the product (subject to marketing practices and macroeconomic factors).

* Actual outcomes necessarily exclude applicants who were denied funds.

The ripple effects of historical discrimination have confounding impacts on economic and social data and is [well documented](https://doi.org/10.1007/s00146-023-01676-3). These impacts are inextricable from data about individual borrowers. 

### Variable Selection

The lending firm’s [initial model evaluation](https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/1jy7Dy4X5INuirYatWkNi3Kd_Z4DTweZt?usp=drive_link) both included and excluded subgroups protected by fair lending regulations. 
* included: age, race, color, sex, marriage status
* included, not protected: education
* excluded: national origin, number of dependents, employment title

A McNemar's test will be carried out for all characteristics within these subgroups to check for inherent bias in the model's logic - positive or negative. 

(e.g. subgroup is gender, characteristic is male and female)

# Analysis Results

The model is highly accurate overall, correctly predicting 22,378 of 22,897 cases: **97.7%**

<img class="post-image" src="{{ '/assets/img/posts/0003a.png' | relative_url }}" alt="confusion matrix">


Of all characteristics within the [8 self-reported subgroups](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1qngfTNbigRDImeYKnBcqpKicLIWVtRM-/view?usp=drive_link), we rejected the null hypothesis for 89%. 

We reject that the algorithm's logic has nothing to do with false assumptions for 25 of the 28 characteristics below.

<img class="post-image" src="{{ '/assets/img/posts/0003b.png' | relative_url }}" alt="characteristic significance">

In the graphic above - the bold grey line for each subgroup represents the threshold where we determine the direction of the bias - towards false positives or false negatives. 27 of the 28 characteristics are on the left side of that threshold, indicating a clear bias towards false negatives.

The algorithm is biased for 89% of the characteristics we assessed, and it assumes borrowers will default most of the time.

# Conclusion

False predictions are likely a result of limited data about default cases. In general, the number of borrowers who default is small. This leads to a predictable imbalance in positive and negative cases.

In cases of uncertainty, the algorithm is more likely to give a false positive than a false negative prediction. This tendency might be the reason why we rejected the null hypothesis for so many characteristics. It is also the reason why the model accuracy is so high.

## Recommendations

Mortgage lenders, policy makers, and academics have been researching the drivers of default for decades, and algorithmic fairness will continue to evolve given the complexity and range of the issues. 

* Use model’s results to inform (vs. decide) lending decisions.

* Continue investment in understanding both the drivers of default and industry methods for fair algorithmic decisions. 

* Increase risk tolerance for default. This would decrease the accuracy of the model, but it would address data limitation issues and provide more insights on the drivers of default.
