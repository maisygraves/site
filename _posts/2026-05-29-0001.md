To become more capable with technical systems, data tools, and AI-assisted workflows, I decided to learn the bare-bones basics of building a website: HTML, CSS, Git, Jekyll, Liquid, YAML, SVG, directories, browser behavior, and all the tiny assumptions underneath them. The experience ended up being more valuable than I could have ever predicted.

## The inspiration

If you're not looking at the output, you're not learning. ## Things I learned

Computers are literal (literally) and you have to tell them everything. Every. Little. Thing. That doesn't just include the look and feel of the webpages, but how the website will build (Jekyll, Liquid, YAML). It is incredibly tedious.

I repeat, computers are literal. However, I don't need to be a senior developer to use them anymore. I don't need to understand binary to create an SVG file, and I don't need to understand SVG to use SVG images in my webpage. Now, thanks to coding agents, I no longer need to have years of experience to create an application.

We stand on the shoulders of giants. Like all tools, computers have a history rooted in solving specific problems. The logical/mathematical roots and hardware/software solutions of the past are reflected in the design of modern tools and the syntax of modern languages. Stay tuned for more notes on this.

There is no substitute for experience. For instance, I wouldn't understand Git without the struggle of making updates across a multi-folder directory, the disatisfaction with changes that took hours to make, and the shame of deleting everything to start over because I didn't understand what merge means. The purpose of a *main* and *development* branch makes sense to me, and I have a healthy respect for proper commit messages in an enterprise setting. I won't say I understand everything about Git, but I have a grasp the basics.

File structure and documentation for personal projects is an essential part of the work - and there were times where I wished the directory structure, naming conventions, code comments, and file structure was cleaner and more intuitive. However, there is also a balance between over-organizing and under-organizing. Making too many liquid templates in the _includes/ folder can grow into a whole other operational nightmare.

The usefulness of my AI prompts and answers improved with my understanding of the components of the system and development process. Having a deeper understanding of the end goal improved my prompts to chatGPT and Claude.

Creating a website is nothing like writing an essay. It's way more efficient to test small things and build upward - trying to build the whole thing from start to finish is a waste of time. Most tutorials recommend breaking tasks down into their atomic parts but I had to learn the hard way that it really is a requirement for success. Also, I find it ironic that staying focused on overarching concepts is what helped me the most here.

## The AI of it all

I already knew that AI tools are more than fancy Google search bars before this project but this experience has unlocked my imagination for what is possible. I have plenty of ideas for things to analyze, build, and learn and now I know how to start. Stay tuned - more to come.
